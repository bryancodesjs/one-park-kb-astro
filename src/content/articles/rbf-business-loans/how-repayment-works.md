<h2>The remittance structure</h2>
<p>Once funded, the client's remittance is set as a fixed percentage of their daily or weekly gross revenue — collected automatically via ACH from their business bank account.</p>

<h2>Example breakdown</h2>
<ul>
  <li>Funded amount: <strong>$50,000</strong></li>
  <li>Factor rate: <strong>1.30</strong></li>
  <li>Total payback: <strong>$65,000</strong></li>
  <li>Daily remittance rate: <strong>~8–12% of daily revenue</strong> (varies by deal)</li>
  <li>Estimated term: <strong>6–12 months</strong> (depends on revenue volume)</li>
</ul>

<div class="tip-box">
  <p class="tip-label">Why the "term" is an estimate</p>
  <p>Because remittance is revenue-based, there's no fixed end date. A business with a great quarter pays off faster. A slower period means smaller payments — but no penalty for it.</p>
</div>

<h2>What happens on a slow day?</h2>
<p>If the client's daily revenue is low, their daily remittance is proportionally lower. This is the core flexibility benefit of RBF vs. a fixed loan payment.</p>

<div class="warning-box">
  <p class="warning-label">ACH and bank account</p>
  <p>Clients must have an active business checking account for ACH remittance. Personal accounts or accounts with frequent overdrafts may cause issues in underwriting.</p>
</div>
